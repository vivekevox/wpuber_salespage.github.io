The Biggest Problem with WordPress

Before you dive into WordPress, try keep a check on your unbridled enthusiasm and ask yourself:

How much do I really know about WordPress? Have I ever worked on a website before? A client’s or a friend’s? How much time am I willing to invest in getting better at WordPress? Do I have time?

Maintaining a website can be overwhelming for a beginner. While there has been an explosion of learning resources dedicated to WordPress, our rate of learning and its ROI isn’t so neat to predict and control. There’s no guarantee that you’ll become a Ninja overnight (and you certainly don’t want to fall into any wishful thinking).

Thinking you can get better at WordPress in a week or a few days is the same as believing that the moon is made of cheese. The moon isn’t made of cheese, and you can’t bend spoons. Sorry. But we digress. Sure, some people do get the hang of it in a week or a few days, but some don’t. And certainly not most.

Some WP developers estimate that learning the nitty-gritty can take at least 100 hours. You can get used to the basics quickly, but when it comes to managing a website (that back-end stuff), you need to know the ins and outs as well as what to do if you crash. We mean if your website crashes. WordPress is a lot more than a WYSIWYG editor.

Learning is expensive, and time isn’t free. And even though there are thousands of free blogs, articles, and video tutorials, the nicer stuff — the well-researched, specific, and actionable resources — is not going to be free.

But what if you’re really good at WordPress. You’re a developer, and you manage other people’s sites. You’re the person folks run to for all things WordPress. But you cannot erase the fact that managing a website isn’t just about money: You will have to, on an average, spend 5-10 hours a month performing routine maintenance tasks such as performing updates, backups, getting rid of spam, and scanning your site for bad links.

The difference between “hanging-there” online businesses and successful online businesses

Many WordPress users believe that deciding to let go of regular maintenance and fix “crashes” with “patches'' as they come is a great cost-effective choice. They assure themselves by believing that their website wouldn’t need much work if they already spent thousands of dollars to have it made in the first place. However, if you want to see your website performing at its very best and cut down on long-term costs, you NEED TO MAINTAIN IT.

Let’s bring that car-cliche out, shall we?

A newly launched WordPress site is like a brand new car. It may work great at first, but over a period of time, it’s going to need some fixing if you want it to last for a few years. Being careless about maintaining your website is both sloppy and irresponsible. You’re just attracting problems and expenses.

For example:

  • You can run into problems frequently if you don’t have strict procedures and monitoring in place.
  • Website costs are unpredictable. Not all WordPress issues are the same. There are so many ways things can go wrong.
  • WordPress updates can be problematic: With compatibility issues, users may have to spend more on expensive redevelopments and website reconfigurations.
  • Every second a penny: If your website goes down, you’re losing traffic and money. If your website is hacked and there has been a data breach, Google could penalize you.

So apart from that White Screen of Death, you can run into these problems that will need your time (an added cost) to troubleshoot. Nothing’s free.

You need to recognize that maintaining a website on a routine basis is a lot cheaper than scrambling and hiring an impromptu developer during an emergency. If you have a WordPress site, know this first: Some of the biggest expenses you’ll have to pay for are not the marketing costs but your sloppiness: Your failure to keep your website safe.

And successful online businesses know this. Every visitor counts, and you want them to come back. But website maintenance isn’t only about security, hackers, and spammers. Your website’s performance, content, and Search Engine Optimization (SEO) could be out of whack, too (as most unmaintained WordPress sites are). Not falling into this mess from the very beginning is the first step.
